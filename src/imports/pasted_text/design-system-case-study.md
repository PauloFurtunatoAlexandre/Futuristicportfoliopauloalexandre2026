Problem
The legacy insurance platform was facing significant user experience challenges. It was a fragmented system with inconsistent design elements, poor accessibility, and a steep learning curve, leaving users, including agents and insured individuals, frustrated. The lack of a standardized design system meant that each business line and product had its own set of rules, causing inefficiencies and difficulties in maintaining the platform. Users often reported confusion, difficulties navigating the system, and a lack of trust in their interactions with the platform when did not receive any feedback from it. Additionally, there was no cohesive strategy to ensure that future updates and expansions would be consistent across the board.

Solution
To address these issues, I led the redesign of both the insured and risk management platforms, working closely with another product designer. We focused on expanding a comprehensive design system that would be scalable, consistent, and user-friendly across all business lines and products. By introducing standardized UI components, design tokens, and detailed guidelines, we created a unified user interface that was both accessible and intuitive. We implemented accessibility standards that adhered to inclusive design principles, making the platform more usable for a wider range of users. Our solution also streamlined the development process, allowing for faster and more efficient updates and maintenance across the entire system.


My Role
As the lead product designer, I played a key role in the transformation of the platform. My primary responsibilities included leading the redesign of the insured and risk management platforms, collaborating with another designer to develop the visual and functional direction. I also mentored four other designers, guiding them through various UX methodologies, design processes, and Figma best practices. Additionally, I took on the responsibility of being the gatekeeper for the design system, ensuring its consistent application across all 33 business lines and products within the company. My leadership ensured that the design system not only solved immediate UX challenges but was also scalable for future growth and expansion.

Using the double-diamond process, I began by identifying the core issues with the legacy insurance platform:
UX Research:
User Interviews: I participate as a Product Designer in collab with an UX Researcher in the interviews with 12 insureds, to uncover their pain points and challenges with the platform.

Desk Research: I reviewed existing analytics, previous user feedback, and performed a internal data collection with our specialists to check points of friction in our experience.



Mapping Pain Points:
After the UX Researcher and I created the Blueprints to map and visualize where users encountered friction, focusing on key tasks such as policy enrollment, risk management, and claims submission, I helped to start grooming the first problems in our UI that would need a redesign and/or a different flow and interactions with users.

Building on the insights from the first diamond, I transitioned into the solution phase:
Idea Generation:
Design Studio Workshop: I facilitated a workshop with cross-functional team members, generating over 30 ideas to address the platform’s usability and consistency issues.

Impact/Effort Matrix: We used the matrix to prioritize the most impactful solutions, such as standardizing UI components, introducing design tokens, and creating a more accessible layout that adhered to inclusive design standards.

Prototyping and Testing:
Wireframes: I created low-fidelity wireframes to map out key workflows and introduce more intuitive designs.

High-Fidelity Prototypes: The wireframes evolved into polished, high-fidelity prototypes that featured:
- Standardized UI Components: Reusable design elements to improve consistency across the platform.
- Design Tokens: Predefined variables for colors, typography, and spacing, ensuring a unified visual identity.
- Accessibility Improvements: Enhancing the layout and interactions to cater to users with disabilities.

User Testing: I conducted usability tests with 12 participants, resulting in a 40% improvement in task completion rates. Feedback led to iterative design refinements, particularly around navigation and form usability.

Challenges and Results
Challenges and Trade-Offs
Balancing User Needs and Business Goals:

One key challenge was balancing the need for a streamlined user experience with the business goal of maintaining diverse features across 33 business lines. We focused on standardizing common tasks and design elements, but some business-specific customizations had to be delayed for future iterations.

Technical Constraints:

Due to the legacy nature of the platform, integrating the design system and making sweeping visual changes was challenging. We implemented the design system incrementally (using our UX Governance processes), focusing first on critical areas such as the insured and risk management platforms, with plans to roll it out across other parts of the system as the architecture allowed.



Results
The redesign and implementation of the new design system led to significant improvements across multiple dimensions:

Accessibility Improvements: With the introduction of more inclusive design practices, the platform became more accessible to users with disabilities. This not only enhanced user experience but also ensured the platform met compliance requirements, reducing potential legal risks.

Efficiency Gains: The design system accelerated development time, as standardized UI components and design tokens allowed for faster iteration across business lines. Teams no longer had to build components from scratch, resulting in a 30% reduction in development time for new features.

Cross-Team Collaboration: The new system created a shared framework that fostered better communication and collaboration between design, development, and product teams. This ensured a consistent user experience across all 33 business lines and products.

Ultimately, the successful implementation of the design system not only solved the platform’s initial challenges but also provided a scalable foundation for future growth, ensuring consistency and efficiency across a diverse product ecosystem.



Next Steps
User Testing Post-Implementation: Conduct additional rounds of user testing with a larger sample size to assess the effectiveness of the design system in real-world use. Using metrics such as task completion rate, time on task, and user satisfaction, we will measure and validate the actual impact of the changes on the user experience (I did not stay in the company to see those results). This will help ensure that the new system is meeting its goals and identify areas for further refinement.

Expand Design System Integration: Continue rolling out the design system across the remaining business lines and products, ensuring consistency across the entire platform. Regularly update the system based on user feedback and emerging design trends to keep it current and effective.

Monitor Accessibility Compliance: Continuously assess and improve the platform’s accessibility to ensure it meets evolving legal standards and user needs. This includes conducting regular audits and integrating assistive technologies where needed.

Iterative Improvements: Based on user feedback and performance metrics, refine and optimize key features of the platform, such as the risk management tools and policy enrollment process, to further streamline user flows and reduce friction.

Stakeholder Alignment: Regularly align with stakeholders across business lines to ensure that the design system evolves in a way that supports business goals while maintaining user-centered design principles. This will foster continued collaboration and help prioritize future enhancements.

By taking these steps, we’ll ensure that the platform continues to evolve in a user-centric, scalable way, with a focus on ongoing improvement and validation.
